Malaysia ranks globally for high vehicle ownership (90% and above).

A steep drop of passengers from 2020 to 2021

The number of passengers gradually increases from 2021 onwards for both intercity and urban rails.

We can also see that most intercity passengers prefer second class seats

Urban Passenger

Urban passenger numbers plummeted in 2021 and have essentially decreased far below their pre-pandemic high. Urban passenger transit remains stagnated, failing to recover even half of its pre-pandemic volume—likely due to the permanent rise of hybrid and remote work.

Intercity Passenger

Intercity passenger numbers experienced the same decrease in 2021 due to the pandemic, however instead of being stagnant, the number of passengers increased and hit above average of 5 years in 2022.

Lack of Rail Transit in East Malaysia

The absence of extensive rail infrastructure in East Malaysia is primarily attributed to its challenging geographical terrain. The region's highly uneven topography and fragmented road networks present significant engineering and logistical obstacles to rail development.

High Density Rail Transit in Peninsular Malaysia

High concentration of rail infrastructure within Peninsular Malaysia, particularly localized inside the Klang Valley. This dense network serves as the backbone for linking high density residential areas as well as working individuals.

Rail connecting North to South Malaysia

The KTM Komuter sectors along the north-south axis plays a definitive role in carbon-mitigation strategy. By offering a high-capacity, electrified transit alternative across multiple states, this enables citizens to be able to travel interstate without needing cars.
